# The Advocacy Gap

The book has a structural blind spot created by the author's own fawning pattern. The book is generous with frameworks, information, and understanding. It's stingy with demands.

This document tracks the pattern and every place it needs to be fixed.

---

## The Pattern

The book's advice to wronged people follows a consistent sequence: **inform → set boundary → accept what you can't control.** The missing step is between "inform" and "accept": **demand action, follow up, escalate, insist on consequences.**

The fawning pattern shows up in three structural ways:

1. **Under-response barely exists.** The book gives 95% of its proportionality content to over-response and 5% to under-response. It's deeply equipped to counsel someone who's too angry and barely equipped to counsel someone who's too quiet.

2. **The wronged person's pages teach informing, not advocating.** "Tell the facilitator your side" but not "tell the facilitator what you want done." "Sometimes there's nothing you can do" before checking "have you actually asked for anything?"

3. **Facilitator failure has no participant-facing recourse.** The facilitator chapters are thorough about what facilitators should do. The participant pages create a dependency on facilitators acting correctly but give zero guidance on what to do when they don't.

## Specific Gaps to Fix

### 1. ~~MAJOR — "The Hardest Part" counsels acceptance before advocacy~~ ✅ FIXED

**File:** `when-youve-been-wronged.md`, lines 191-214

"Sometimes there's nothing you can do" jumps straight to acceptance without checking: have you demanded anything? Have you told the facilitator what you want? Have you insisted on consequences? The "what you can't control" list includes "whether you get an apology" — but demanding one IS in your control. Whether they comply isn't.

**Fix:** Add a step before acceptance — "Before you accept that nothing can be done, check: have you actually asked for what you want?" Move "The Hardest Part" to AFTER the reader has been told to advocate.

---

### 2. MAJOR — Under-response barely explored in appropriate-response.md

**File:** `appropriate-response.md`

Under-response gets two lines out of 305. The entire page — flowcharts, examples, self-checks, common mistakes — is about over-response. No content exists for: warning signs you're under-responding, how fawning distorts response downward, what under-response looks like (facilitators who don't act, wronged people who don't speak up), what to do about it.

**Fix:** Add a section on under-response with equivalent depth. Warning signs, examples, self-checks. Mirror the over-response content.

### 3. ~~MAJOR — "Talk to Facilitators" says inform, not advocate~~ ✅ FIXED

**File:** `when-youve-been-wronged.md`, lines 169-179

The section tells the reader to "let the facilitator know your side." It never says: tell them what you want done, demand proportional consequences, follow up if they don't act, confront them if they're fawning.

The last line — "If the facilitator only listens to the accuser, that tells you something about the facilitator" — counsels noting it as data rather than confronting it. Textbook fawning.

**Fix:** Expand to include: what to ask for, how to demand action, what to do when the facilitator doesn't act. Connect to existing content in `guiding-public-repair.md` lines 262-281 where a participant confronts a fawning facilitator.

### 4. ~~MAJOR — "The Way Out" defines power as stopping harm, not asking for repair~~ ✅ FIXED

**File:** `when-youve-been-wronged.md`, lines 238-492

The section's climax lists what you can do: "say no and leave," "enforce boundaries," "stop absorbing abuse." All defensive. None are: "demand an apology," "demand the facilitator enforce consequences," "demand a public correction." Power is defined as the ability to leave, not the ability to receive what you're owed.

**Fix:** Add advocacy actions to the power list. "You can demand repair. You can demand the facilitator do their job. You can demand proportional consequences."

### 5. ~~MAJOR — Facilitator failure has no participant recourse~~ ✅ PARTIALLY FIXED (covered in expanded "Talk to Facilitators" section; cross-references to guiding-public-repair.md still TODO)

All three pages tell the reader to "tell facilitators," "involve facilitators," "report to facilitators." None address: what do you do when the facilitator doesn't act? Excellent content about confronting facilitator passivity exists in `guiding-public-repair.md` lines 262-281 — but it's buried in a facilitator-oriented chapter that a wronged participant would never find.

**Fix:** Add guidance to participant-facing pages about recognizing facilitator fawning, confronting it, and what recourse exists when facilitators fail. Cross-reference the existing content in `guiding-public-repair.md`.

### 7. MODERATE — "When Repair Isn't Possible" counsels passivity

**File:** `making-it-right.md`, lines 248-258

When the wrongdoer won't take responsibility, the advice is: match severity, inform facilitators, stay safe, don't retaliate. All passive/informing. The final bullet — "don't become a harm-creator yourself" — prioritizes the reader's restraint before their advocacy.

**Fix:** Add: demand the facilitator enforce consequences even without the wrongdoer's cooperation. Insist on proportional accountability whether or not the person wants to repair.

### 8. MODERATE — Follow-up with mistake-maker gives information, doesn't ask what they need

**File:** `handling-threats-of-violence.md`, lines 176-188

The mistake-maker was the victim of death threats — and the follow-up treats them as someone to be informed, not someone whose demands should be heard.

**Fix:** Add "ask them what they need" to the follow-up protocol.

## The Underlying Insight

The book reflects the author at the time of writing. The author's fawning pattern — giving frameworks instead of demanding repair, informing instead of advocating, accepting instead of insisting — created a book that's brilliant at understanding harm and weak at demanding justice.

The fix isn't adding a section. It's adding a *voice* — the voice that says "you deserve repair, and here's how to demand it" with the same authority the book uses to say "here's how to see clearly."

This is a multi-page project that touches the core of the wronged-person experience in the book.
