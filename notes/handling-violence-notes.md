# Handling Threats of Violence — Complete Notes

Notes from conversation on Mar 4, 2026. Source material for `src/concepts/handling-threats-of-violence.md`.

**Every point below needs to be addressed in the page.**

---

## The Core Scenario

- Someone makes a good-faith mistake (protected under the promise). Another person escalates to death threats / threats of violence.
- The facilitator's job is to resolve this safely, not morally. Safety first, then accountability, then communication.

---

## Order of Operations

1. Remove the mistake-maker FIRST — not as punishment, but because the facilitator deems the situation unsafe for them.
2. If you ask the violent person to leave first, they might lash out and attack the mistake-maker before they can get away.
3. Keep the violent person contained until the mistake-maker is safely out of range (gone home, traveled far enough they can't be followed).
4. Then remove the violent person for violating the container's agreements.
5. Then address the crowd — explain what happened and why, after both parties are gone.

---

## What NOT to Do (Both Are Fawning)

- **Do not remove the mistake-maker and keep the violent person.** This teaches everyone that mistakes get you expelled and death threats are tolerated. The angry person is now running the container. This is fawning — you're afraid of the violent person so you sacrifice the easier target.
- **Do not do nothing.** Also fawning. Pretending death threats didn't happen because you're afraid to confront the violent person. Extremely dangerous.
- Both of these are facilitator fawning — avoiding confrontation with the threatening person out of fear, at the expense of the person who made a mistake.

---

## The Gap Between Removals

- This is the most dangerous and turbulent window of the entire scenario.
- The crowd sees you removing the mistake-maker and NOT (yet) the violent person — which looks like you're violating the promise.
- The violent person is still in the room and might try to rally support, spin their story, or escalate.
- **Pre-framing solution:** At the start of the container, pre-frame that in emergency situations, rules may be acted on in a certain order or with facilitator discretion to keep things safe. Staff might not explain every action immediately because it's a matter of necessity.
- **In-the-moment language:** "We're in emergency protocols. This will be addressed momentarily. Please bear with us. The promise will be completely fulfilled and honored — soon, and definitely by the end of this container."
- Participants are right to point out if facilitators aren't following their own rules — and you should welcome that. But in emergencies, you might say "you're right to flag that, and I will address it, but I need you to trust me for the next few minutes."
- **Having a first officer / co-facilitator is critical here.** One person escorts the mistake-maker out. The other stays with the group and the violent person. Trying to do this solo leaves dangerous gaps.

---

## What to Say to Each Party

### To the Mistake-Maker (When Asking Them to Leave)

- "You're leaving not because your mistake wasn't okay — those are protected — but because I deem this situation unsafe for you right now. This is an emergency, and I'm acting to protect you."
- Make it absolutely clear: their mistake is still protected. They are not being punished. This is a safety action.

### To the Violent Person (When Removing Them)

- "I can see you're in a lot of pain right now, and I take that seriously. AND threats of violence are outside the agreements of this container. You need to leave. We can talk about what happened after you've had time to come down, but right now, you cannot be here."
- Not moralizing. Not "fuck you, get out." But firm, clear, and showing you want to support them without tolerating the behavior.
- This is what would actually make them reflect on their actions — as opposed to keeping them there because you're fawning, not telling them they did anything wrong.

### To the Crowd (After Both Have Left)

- The mistake-maker left not because they made a mistake — mistakes are protected — but because threats were being made against their life and the facilitator deemed it an unsafe situation.
- After the mistake-maker was safely away, the violent person was asked to leave because threats of violence violate the container's agreements and will not be tolerated.
- The facilitator is acting impartially. This is about safety, not morality. Not picking favorites.
- Frame the violent person as someone who was in pain AND crossed a line — both things true simultaneously. Don't moralize them into a villain.

---

## Refund Policy (Show Don't Tell)

- **REFUND the mistake-maker** who had to leave for their own safety. Shows through action (not just words) that mistakes are actually accepted and what happened was abnormal. A "thank you for leaving when I asked you to so I could keep the container safe."
- **REFUND the violent person's romantic partner** or any innocent party connected to them who has to leave through no fault of their own.
- **Do NOT refund the violent person.** They violated the agreements.
- The differential refunding sets an extremely sharp precedent — it actually communicates (not just says) what's okay and what's not.
- It disrupts the "evil predator was expelled" story the crowd might form.

### Should You Tell the Crowd About Refunds?

- **Arguments for telling:** Shows you're serious. Shows that the mistake-maker isn't leaving because they made a mistake. Gives the crowd trust and safety — "if I make a mistake, I'm actually protected, even in worst case." Shows them HOW to handle bad situations (teaching, not just modeling). Shows through action, not just words.
- **Arguments against:** Might be too much information. Financial details could feel unprofessional.
- **Lean:** Probably tell them. It's showing instead of telling, and it teaches people how things should be handled, which is valuable beyond just this one incident.
- Also consider telling them you refunded the violent person's partner but NOT the violent person — this further sharpens the precedent.

---

## Follow-Up After the Event

### With the Mistake-Maker

- Reach out to check on them. "How are you?"
- Make clear they're welcome back to FUTURE containers (NOT the same ongoing retreat — the violent person could figure out they're there and come back).
- **Tell them how you and your organization are handling the violent person.** What boundaries you're setting, what consequences are being enforced. This is extremely important — they need to know you took action, not just moved on.
- **Safety planning:** Does the violent person live in the mistake-maker's area? If yes, the mistake-maker needs to take steps to make sure the violent person can't find their address through word of mouth, can't come attack or harass them, can't slander their reputation in their local community. The facilitator should proactively flag this risk.
- If the violent person is eventually allowed back into future containers/events, **notify the mistake-maker** so they can check before attending. Ideally the mistake-maker takes responsibility for checking themselves (asking facilitators "is this person attending?"), because facilitators can't always track every conflict pair across every event.

### With the Violent Person

- Clear communication about whether they're welcome at future events, and under what conditions (see re-entry policy below).
- Not moralizing — but firm and clear.

---

## Violent Person Re-Entry Policy

- **Blacklisted from re-entering containers** until EITHER:
  - Sufficient time has passed (e.g., a year), OR
  - They've undergone an actual repair process
- "Repair" means not just apologizing but **actually making it right** — to the facilitators, to the container, AND especially to the person they threatened.
- If they haven't seen through their story ("I was right, there was a dangerous predator, I protected everyone"), they'll do the same thing in future containers. Their nervous system is tuned to see threats and predators that aren't there and they'll cause the same problems.
- **Boundary example:** "You're out of our containers for a year. We can talk about coming back then. This is not acceptable and you violated our agreements. Go cool off and think about it."
- Setting this boundary SHOWS (not tells) that death threats aren't acceptable — as opposed to doing nothing.
- **The bad outcome (real example):** When the violent person faces zero consequences, stays in the container, enjoys the event, and even offers to build a facility for hosting future events — and the facilitator says okay without addressing that the violent person can't be trusted to have that kind of power. This is catastrophically wrong. It tells everyone: violence works, the violent person is in charge, and the facilitator is afraid of them.

### Should You Tell the Crowd About Re-Entry Policy?

- Tell them the violent person isn't welcome back until repair is actually done (especially to the person they threatened).
- This shows everyone: this is what happens when you make threats of violence. We're serious. You are safe. We won't expose you to that unsafety in future containers.

---

## Pre-Framing (Beginning of Container)

- Pre-frame that mistakes are okay (already in the book — the promise).
- Pre-frame how emergencies will be handled: "In emergency situations, we may act in a certain order or with discretion that might not be immediately obvious. We might not explain ourselves at every moment because safety requires speed. But we will explain afterward, and the promise will be honored."
- This gives people a sense of "oh, this is what's supposed to happen" when things go wrong, instead of panic and stories.

---

## Staff/Assistant Mistakes

- Same framework applies — mistakes are protected regardless of role. Staff are just expected to make fewer mistakes.
- Pre-framing that staff can make mistakes: frame it as credibility, not vulnerability. "Our staff are trained and held to high standards. They're also human. If a staff member makes a mistake, it's handled with the same framework."
- The person who says "nothing will ever go wrong" hasn't thought about failure modes and is less trustworthy than the one with a plan. (Like pilots doing safety briefings — nobody gets off the plane because the flight attendant mentions emergency exits.)
- When there's a staff mistake WITHOUT death threats: consider whether staff should have harder discipline (not punishment) — e.g., "don't play in the play space tonight." This shows the container takes staff responsibility seriously. Participants might still be able to play after the same kind of mistake. This acknowledges the power differential and higher responsibility without being punitive.
- Open question: how to handle the power differential with care. Staff are held to higher standards but still human.

---

## "Nothing Will Go Wrong" — Facilitator Version

- Already have content about participants who say "I would never do that" being most likely to hurt others.
- Same pattern applies to facilitators who say "nothing will ever go wrong" — they haven't thought about failure modes, which makes them LESS safe.
- The facilitator who has a plan for when things go wrong is more trustworthy than the one who claims things won't go wrong.

## Don't Do This Alone

- The first officer concept (already a section in the book) is critical in emergency scenarios.
- One person handles the removal logistics, the other manages the room.
- Without a co-facilitator, there are dangerous gaps where the violent person is unsupervised or the crowd is unmanaged.

---

## The Violent Person Is Also Human

- They're probably triggered, flooded, in a trauma response. That doesn't excuse the behavior or change the consequence (they leave).
- Frame them to the group as someone who was in pain AND crossed a line — both things true simultaneously.
- Don't moralize them into a villain. This is consistent with the book's core stance: describe behavior, not character.

---

## Aftercare for the Container

- After both parties have left and the crowd has been addressed, the container's energy needs attention.
- People may be shaken, scared, questioning whether they're safe.
- The facilitator's transparent communication about what happened and why is itself a form of aftercare.
- The refund disclosure, the re-entry policy disclosure, and the clear enforcement of the promise all serve to restore container trust.
- Important: this is a genuinely shit situation. Even handled perfectly, it's disruptive. The goal isn't to make it painless — it's to make it clear, fair, and safe.
